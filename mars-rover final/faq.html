Everything you wanted to know about Mars, the rovers, and NASA's exploration program.

Common questions about the rovers, the science, and future missions to Mars.

About the Rovers

What is the difference between Curiosity and Perseverance?

Both are car-sized rovers, but they have different goals. Curiosity (launched 2011) was designed to determine whether Mars ever had conditions suitable for microbial life. Perseverance (launched 2020) takes the next step — actively looking for signs of ancient life, collecting rock samples for eventual return to Earth, and testing technology for future human missions.

How big are the rovers?

Both Curiosity and Perseverance are about the size of a car. They each weigh roughly 1,000 kg (about 2,200 lbs) on Earth and measure approximately 3 meters long, 2.7 meters wide, and 2.2 meters tall. Their six-wheel design allows them to roll over rocks up to 40 cm (16 inches) tall.

Are the rovers still active today?

Yes — as of 2026, both Curiosity and Perseverance are still operational and transmitting data back to Earth. Curiosity has been active for over 13 years, far exceeding its original 2-year primary mission. Perseverance continues to collect rock core samples and conduct science experiments in Jezero Crater.

The Science

Has Mars ever had liquid water?

Yes — both rovers have confirmed this. Curiosity found ancient lake sediments and mineral deposits in Gale Crater consistent with a long-standing freshwater lake billions of years ago. Perseverance landed in Jezero Crater, which was once a river delta and lake bed. The geological evidence strongly supports that liquid water flowed on Mars for extended periods early in its history.

Have the rovers found signs of life?

Not definitively — not yet. Curiosity found complex organic molecules and seasonal methane fluctuations, which are interesting but not proof of life. Perseverance has collected rock core samples that may contain biosignatures (signs of ancient life), but those samples need to be returned to Earth for detailed laboratory analysis before any conclusion can be made. The Mars Sample Return mission is planned for this purpose.

What is the MOXIE experiment?

MOXIE stands for Mars Oxygen In-Situ Resource Utilization Experiment. It's a device aboard Perseverance that successfully produced breathable oxygen from Mars's carbon dioxide atmosphere in 2021. This is a critical technology demonstration for future human missions — future astronauts could use a scaled-up version to produce both oxygen to breathe and rocket propellant for the return trip to Earth.

What happened to the Ingenuity helicopter?

Ingenuity was the first aircraft to achieve powered, controlled flight on another planet. Originally planned for just 5 flights as a technology test, it went on to complete 72+ flights over nearly three years. In January 2024, Ingenuity damaged a rotor blade during its final landing and was no longer able to fly. NASA declared its mission complete, but its legacy proved that aerial exploration of Mars is possible for future missions.

Missions Future

How long does it take to travel from Earth to Mars?

It depends on the launch window, but typically around 6 to 9 months. Spacecraft must launch during a specific window every ~26 months when Earth and Mars are properly aligned. Both Curiosity and Perseverance took approximately 7 months to reach Mars. The distance between the two planets varies from about 54 million km to 400 million km depending on their orbital positions.

Will the Mars rock samples ever come back to Earth?

That is the plan. NASA and ESA (European Space Agency) are working on the Mars Sample Return mission, which would send a lander to retrieve Perseverance's sealed rock core tubes and launch them into Mars orbit, where another spacecraft would collect them and bring them back to Earth. This would be the first time material from Mars has been returned for study in Earth laboratories, where far more powerful analysis tools are available.

Will humans ever go to Mars?

NASA and private companies like SpaceX are working toward crewed Mars missions. The rovers are helping pave the way — Curiosity measured radiation levels to understand health risks for future astronauts, while Perseverance is testing oxygen production and identifying safe landing zones. A crewed mission would likely take 2-3 years round-trip. No firm launch date has been set, but both NASA and SpaceX have expressed goals in the 2030s-2040s timeframe.
